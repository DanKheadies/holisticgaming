Abstract

Video games are media-rich tools that make learning more accessible for individuals with cognitive disabilities.

Video games and video game play make learning more accessible by presenting information in multiple ways, providing several ways to plan and perform tasks, and by motivating and engaging players. This style of learning facilitates all types of learners, and individuals with cognitive disabilities can use video game play to treat and train their mental deficiencies. A person with a cognitive disability has difficulty using mental abilities to execute mental tasks, such as sustaining attention, remembering information, comprehending text, and so on. Cognitive disabilities range in severity and diagnosis, and in order to be effective, treatment needs to affect both neurological structures as well as psychological behaviors1. Fortunately, video games affect physical and cognitive domains, and they are flexible and sophisticated enough to accommodate an array of cognitive disabilities.

Video games are media-rich tools that provide equal opportunities for players to train and develop mental abilities. Video games present information in a number of ways:

  • Visual and auditory cues
  • Repeatable experiences
  • Logs and menus
  • Short-term objectives and long-term goals
  • Stories and adventures
  • Real-time action vs. turn-based strategy
  • Scalable difficulty
  • Individual and multiplayer game play
  • Competition and cooperation
  • And in other various ways

Video games and video game play make information interactive, which allows a player to recognize and understand that information in a personal way. Players learn, plan, and execute physical and mental tasks in order to achieve goals and objectives. Each action in a video game is coupled with immediate feedback, and this feedback promotes deliberate learning and mastery towards the task at hand2. Video game play is naturally challenging and rewarding, and video games engage and motivate players to invest time and energy in that video game play experience3,4,5,6. By using these innate characteristics of video games, we can entice players to exercise and train cognitive skills, which can help treat a number of cognitive disabilities7.

By nature or by nurture, some individuals possess limited or severely limited cognitive abilities. A number of neurological and behavior factors contribute to clinical conditions, such as attention deficit disorder (ADD), severe clinical conditions, like traumatic brain injury (TBI), and functional disabilities that deal with specific cognitive domains, such as memory deficits7. Cognitive disabilities can be broken down into functional components that define an individual's limitations, and we can train those limitations through video games and video game play.

Cognition is defined as the mental processes associated with memory, language, perception, attention, and problem solving, decision-making, and reasoning8. Cognition develops through bottom-up and top-down processes. The bottom-up process is input and data driven, which involves perceiving the world, remembering features and characteristics about it, and navigating the environment. The top-down process applies previously learned knowledge to affect perception, memory, problem solving, decision-making, and the like8. Video game play challenges these process and drives cognitive development. Through a cyclical flow of bottom-up and top-down processing, a player perceives and interacts with the video game environment--processing bottom-up--and in order to succeed in the game, s/he must solve problems and use several cognitive skills to overcome obstacles and challenges--processing top-down.

Multiple observations report cognitive growth from video game play9,10,11,12,13. This supports the idea that video games train and develop cognitive abilities, and video game play can provide a treatment as well as prevention option to cognitive disabilities. I purpose that we can study the affect of video game play on cognitive domains, and through extensive research, we can design video game systems to accommodate cognitive needs at an individual level. In order to do that, I will investigate and experiment with a holistic game: Immunis. Immunis is a real-time strategy game where in a player experiences and directs an immune response. The player controls several leukocytes, i.e. white blood cells with unique abilities, and defends the body from an infection. As a holistic game, Immunis will:

  1. Provide educational value and pedagogical support for immunology specific information
  2. Help prevent illnesses, e.g. lung cancer, by making the player cognizant of how these illnesses arise as well as the damage they cause
  3. Assess cognitive growth and development
