VIDEO
GAMES &
LEARNING

Video games and video game play create an ideal environment for learning. They present information in a variety of ways, scale in difficulty and complexity, and are customizable for a number of physical and cognitive disabilities. Video games and video game play create a personal and meaningful experience that engages and motivates learners to interact with the information presented to them.

Humans have played games for thousands of years1. Through natural events and environmental influences, games and play have affected humans physically and mentally, and they continue to affect us today1. From physical sports like the Olympics to mental challenges like solitare, games and play have entwined with our society and culture1. Across the world, people of all ages play games for a variety of reasons, and as humans grow and develop, games and play develop too. Within this last century, humans have transformed games and play into video games and video game play. These technological advancements come in many shapes, colors, and flavors. They are played on computers, in arcades, on consoles, and mobile devices with various types of inputs and outputs2,3. These technological innovations make our world fun, frustrating, and full of potential. Several fields--education, medicine, business--are incorporating gamic properties as well as video games into their practices because play and video game play are enjoyable, biologically encouraged, provide immediate feedback, and they motivate exploration and learning1,3,4,5. This relationship between games and play affects several areas in the brain:6,7,8,9
  • Sensory (or Recognition) Regions
    • the visual cortex
    • the auditory cortex
    • the somatosensory cortex
  • Decision and Execution (or Strategy) Regions
    • the premotor cortex
    • the motor cortex
    • the prefrontal cortex
  • Reward Centers of the Brain (or Affective Networks)
    • the basal ganglia
    • the limbic system
    • structures in the medial forebrain circuit
Since individuals vary in physical and psychological abilities, learners recognize information differently, they respond and solve problems differently, and they have different levels of engagement and motivation10. Video games and video game play provide a flexible approach to learning because their content and controls can be customized for individual needs. If a person's learning is limited by serious conditions, i.e. a mental disability or physical aliment, video games can be designed and integrated with assistive, adaptive, or rehabilitative techonology. For example, individuals that struggle with attention and focusing tasks can train these abilities with video game neurofeedback, which intergrates and connects elements within the game, such as speed or power, to neurological frequencies emitted by the player11. If a person's abilities are limited by their environment, such as social isolation or a lack of purpose, play and video game play can 'turn on' players by facilitating learner involvement, direction, and drive. As a few examples, video games are used to create and optimize professional skills such as learning accouting, economic, and business lessons in World of Warcraft; video games are used in classrooms to personalize course material in a 'hands-on' experience, like playing Minecraft to teach students different styles of governance, learn how to negotiate, and to develop social skills; and gamic elements are being used to provide students with feedback, motivation, and engagment, such as through quest-based learning (see images below). Through innovations like these, society will be able to develop video games and use video game play to facilitate education, provide prevention and treatment for a number of disabilities, train professionals, and as research to continue to understand humans.
